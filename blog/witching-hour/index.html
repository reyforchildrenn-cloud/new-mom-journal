The Witching Hour: Why Evenings Are the Hardest Part of the Day

Evenings are hardest for a reason that has nothing to do with anything you did wrong. The witching hour, that stretch of intense fussiness typically between 5pm and 11pm, usually starts around two weeks of age and tends to ease by around twelve weeks, peaking for most families around the six-week mark.

Why it happens

By late afternoon, a newborn's nervous system has absorbed a full day of sights, sounds, and new sensations and is simply maxed out, much the same way an adult feels frayed after a long, overstimulating day. Many babies also naturally cluster feed in the evening, tanking up on shorter, more frequent feeds before settling into their longest stretch of nighttime sleep, which adds to the fussiness during exactly this window.

Strategies that actually help

  • Dim the lights earlier. Reducing stimulation before the fussiness typically starts can soften its intensity.
  • Try babywearing. The motion and closeness of being worn calms many overstimulated babies faster than being set down.
  • Start the evening routine early. Beginning bath time or calming activities before your baby typically becomes fussy can shift the whole evening.
  • Manage your own state too. Babies pick up on caregiver stress, staying as calm as you can, even if it feels forced, tends to help your baby settle faster.

It is temporary and nearly universal

You cannot fully prevent the witching hour in most babies, since it is a normal part of an immature nervous system finding its footing, but you can reduce its intensity and duration with the strategies above. It is temporary, predictable, and experienced by the vast majority of families. Most find it fades on its own well before the three-month mark, often without any single trick being responsible.

A simple evening routine to build around

Many families find success shifting the whole evening earlier: dim lights and reduce noise by late afternoon, offer a feed before your baby seems desperately hungry, and move bath or a calming wind-down routine up rather than waiting until the usual fussy window has already started. Starting the sequence before the storm, rather than reacting once it begins, tends to blunt its intensity more than any single soothing technique used mid-meltdown.

Frequently asked questions

What age does the witching hour start and end?

It typically begins around two weeks of age, peaks around six weeks, and fades by roughly twelve weeks for most babies.

Is witching hour the same as colic?

They can look similar, but colic is defined as crying more than three hours a day, three days a week, for three weeks, and is generally more intense and harder to soothe than typical evening fussiness.

Could the witching hour actually be colic?

They can look similar, but colic is typically defined as crying for more than three hours a day, more than three days a week, for more than three weeks, and tends to be more intense and harder to soothe than typical evening fussiness. If crying seems inconsolable and prolonged well beyond a predictable evening window, mention it to your pediatrician.

Does what I eat affect my baby's evening fussiness if I am breastfeeding?

For most babies, no clear link exists, though a small number of babies do react to specific foods like dairy in a mother's diet. If fussiness is severe and paired with other symptoms like blood in stool or significant reflux, it is worth discussing with your pediatrician rather than eliminating foods on your own first.

Is it okay to just let my baby cry for a few minutes during this hour?

In the newborn stage, responding to crying is generally recommended rather than a structured wait, since young infants are not developmentally able to self-soothe. A few minutes while you finish an urgent task is not harmful, but this window is not the stage for sleep training approaches aimed at older babies.

Does the witching hour come back later in the first year?

Occasionally, in a milder form during later developmental leaps or teething, but the intense, near-daily version tied to an immature nervous system is specific to the newborn period and reliably fades by around three months for most babies.
