The First 72 Hours of Breastfeeding: A Survival Guide

The first three days of breastfeeding are, by a wide margin, the steepest learning curve you will face with your baby, and almost nobody tells you that in advance. Everything from positioning to timing to your own recovery is happening at once, for the first time, with no practice runs.

Hour by hour, roughly

In the first hours after birth, colostrum is all that is available, and that is exactly enough for a stomach the size of a marble. Skin-to-skin contact in this window helps regulate your baby's temperature, heart rate, and blood sugar, while also encouraging early feeding cues. By day two, engorgement can begin to set in as milk starts transitioning, making a latch that felt fine on day one suddenly feel different as your breasts fill and firm up.

8-12
feeds a day is normal for a newborn in the first 72 hours, often in unpredictable, tightly spaced clusters rather than a neat schedule.

Day two and three

By day three, many mothers notice their milk has come in, breasts feeling fuller and firmer, sometimes uncomfortably so, right as your baby is also getting more efficient at nursing and starting to transfer more milk per feed. This is also typically when the newborn's stomach capacity grows from the size of a marble to roughly a ping pong ball, allowing for slightly longer stretches between feeds, though still frequent by adult standards.

Expect feeding sessions that do not follow a predictable clock in these early days. Cluster feeding, particularly in the evening, is common even in the first 72 hours, as babies work to establish supply and mothers work to recognize hunger cues they have never had to read before.

None of this means you are doing it wrong

None of this is a sign you are doing it wrong. It is the normal, uncomfortable process of two bodies learning to work together, and it gets measurably easier by the end of week one for the vast majority of mothers. If something feels off, persistent pain that does not ease within a feed, a baby who seems unable to latch at all, or signs of dehydration, do not wait out the 72 hours alone, ask a nurse, lactation consultant, or pediatrician on staff before you are discharged, while expert help is still just down the hall.

What your own recovery looks like at the same time

While your baby is learning to feed, your own body is doing significant work too. Uterine cramping, sometimes called afterpains, is common in the first 72 hours as your uterus begins contracting back down, and it is often more noticeable with each subsequent baby. Vaginal soreness or a healing perineal tear, or incision pain after a c-section, layer on top of the sleep deprivation and hormonal shifts already happening, which is part of why this window feels so intense on every front at once.

Frequently asked questions

Is it normal to not produce much milk in the first 72 hours?

Yes, colostrum, not full milk volume, is what your body produces in these early hours, and it is exactly the right amount for a newborn's tiny stomach.

When should I call the hospital or doctor in the first 72 hours?

Heavy bleeding, a fever, severe headache, or a baby with very few wet diapers and extreme sleepiness at feeds all warrant an immediate call rather than waiting for a scheduled visit.

Is it normal to feel completely overwhelmed in the first 72 hours?

Extremely normal. You are recovering physically, adjusting hormonally, and learning an entirely new skill simultaneously, with very little sleep. Feeling overwhelmed in this window is close to universal, not a sign anything is wrong.

How often should skin-to-skin contact happen in these early days?

As often as is practical. Skin-to-skin in the first hours and repeated through the following days helps regulate your baby's temperature and heart rate, encourages feeding cues, and has been shown to support easier early breastfeeding for many mother-baby pairs.

When should I expect my milk to fully come in?

Typically between day two and five, with day three being common, marked by breasts feeling noticeably fuller and firmer than the colostrum-only days before it.

What is genuinely an emergency in the first 72 hours versus normal discomfort?

Heavy bleeding soaking a pad within an hour, a fever, severe headache with vision changes, or a baby with very few wet diapers and extreme sleepiness at feeds are signs to call your care team right away rather than waiting for a scheduled follow-up.
