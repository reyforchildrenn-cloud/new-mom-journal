Real talk on pregnancy, breastfeeding, and life with your newborn.

Becoming a mother, whether it's your first time or your third, comes with more questions than anyone prepares you for. Will I know what to do? Is this normal? Am I doing enough? If you're pregnant, newly postpartum, or somewhere in the exhausting, beautiful middle of it, you're not alone in wondering.

The nights blur together. Your body feels unfamiliar in ways no one fully warned you about. Somebody hands you a baby and expects you to just know what to do — and somehow, most days, you figure it out anyway. Slowly, the exhausted middle-of-the-night moments start to feel less frightening and a little more like yours.

This is a space for the parts of new motherhood nobody puts on a registry card: the worry, the healing, the small wins nobody sees, the help you didn't know how to ask for. No judgment, no overwhelm, just honest, practical guidance for the questions that actually keep you up at night — and the people who show up for you while you figure it out.

Why We Have to Talk About This

We feel like we have to bring this up, because no mother should have to go through it in silence. Of everything that catches new mothers off guard in the first weeks of breastfeeding, one physical struggle comes up more than any other, far more often than anyone prepares you for.

As many as 96% of breastfeeding mothers experience sore, painful nipples, especially in that first week, and roughly one in three go on to develop actual cracked, broken skin within the first month. This isn't a rare complication. It is closer to the default experience, and yet it is almost never talked about before it happens.

of breastfeeding mothers report sore or painful nipples, most often in the first week, before the skin and the latch have had time to adjust to each other.

It happens because nothing about this is broken, it is simply new. Skin that has never done this before is being asked to do it eight to twelve times a day, right as milk is coming in and engorgement is making everything feel more sensitive. The pain itself is common and explainable, but the isolation around it doesn't have to be. Every mother deserves to know, before it happens, that this is coming, that it is not a sign anything is wrong, and that there are real, gentle ways to get through it.

Sore Nipples in the First Week: What's Normal and What's Not

Almost every new mother feels it somewhere between the second and fifth feeding: a raw, tender ache that makes the next latch something to brace for. If that's where you are right now, you're not doing anything wrong — your body and your baby are both still learning.

Some tenderness in the first week is normal. Your nipples have never been asked to do this before, and a few days of adjustment is expected even with a good latch. What isn't normal is pain that makes you dread feeding, or skin that cracks, bleeds, or blisters.

9/10
new mothers report some nipple tenderness in the first two weeks of breastfeeding. For most, it fades on its own well before the one-month mark.

What a normal latch feels like

A comfortable latch should feel like tugging, not pinching. If the pain is sharp and lasts through the whole feed, it's worth checking your baby's position before anything else — most early pain traces back to a latch that's shallow rather than deep.

Three things that actually help this week

  • Fix the latch first. A lactation consultant can watch one feed and catch what's hard to see in the mirror.
  • Let them air-dry. Trapped moisture slows healing more than almost anything else.
  • Protect the skin between feeds. Whatever you use, the goal is the same — give the skin a chance to recover before the next latch.

By the end of the first two weeks, most soreness fades on its own as both of you get the hang of it. If it's getting worse instead of better, or you notice cracking or bleeding, that's the point to call a lactation consultant rather than wait it out.
